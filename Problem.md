# Most Relevant Recent Study
Bates et al. (2023) — "The Safety of Inpatient Health Care"
New England Journal of Medicine

What Happened:
Harvard researchers reviewed over 2,800 patient records across 11 U.S. hospitals to measure how much harm patients experience during hospital stays — the most rigorous such analysis in decades.
Key Findings:

Adverse events occurred in nearly 1 in 4 hospital admissions, and roughly 1 in 4 of those events were preventable. PubMed
The top causes of harm were medication errors (39%), surgical/procedural errors (30%), nursing care issues like falls (15%), and hospital-acquired infections (12%). LinkedIn
Approximately 400,000 hospitalized patients experience some form of preventable harm each year. NCBI

Reference:
Bates, D. W., Levine, D. M., Salmasian, H., et al. (2023). The safety of inpatient health care. New England Journal of Medicine, 388(2), 142–153.
🔗 https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMsa2206117

# Dental Training — Nerve Damage from Wrong Angles

In dental schools (like cases reported at University of Pacific and schools across India), student dentists practicing root canal procedures on mannequins have drilled at incorrect angles because the instructor was busy with other students. When these students moved to real patients during clinical rotations, some caused inferior alveolar nerve damage, leading to permanent numbness in patients' lips and chin. Dental schools then invested in expensive haptic simulators (₹50-80 lakh each, like Simodont) to solve this — but a camera-based system could be 100x cheaper.

# Electrical/Electronics Lab — Soldering Burns & Cold Joints
In engineering colleges across India (and incidents reported at IITs and NITs), students in electronics labs frequently produce cold solder joints because they hold the soldering iron at the wrong angle or don't maintain contact for the required dwell time (typically 3-5 seconds). Some students also get burns because of incorrect hand positioning. Instructors monitoring 40 students in a lab simply cannot catch every error.
Connection: Your system could track the hand's spatial position relative to the PCB, check the angle of grip, and ensure the student holds the position for the minimum dwell time.

# The Core Study on Operating Time & Tremor
Slack, P. S., et al. (2008). The effect of operating time on surgeon's hand tremor. European Archives of Otorhinolaryngology, 266(1), 137–141.

🔗 https://doi.org/10.1007/s00405-008-0714-9

What Happened & Key Findings:
Operating led to an increase in hand tremor in all subjects, and tremor increases were directly proportional to the length of time spent operating.
